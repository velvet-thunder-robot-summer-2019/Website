Mechanical Design

Chassis

As the chassis supports the arm, the stone storage mechanism, the circuitry and the drivetrain, as well as having to withstand collisions by other robots, sturdiness and stability was prioritized. To this end, the chassis was made as wide and long as the competition rules would allow. 3mm hardboard was used for most of its construction as it is light, relatively hard and insulating.

The chassis is 3-wheeled to ensure that the position of the chassis is fully defined even when the robot is placed awkwardly. This prevents the driving wheels of the robot from losing contact with the ground.

Each of the 2 driving wheels is powered by a Geared Barber Coleman motor, but geared up with a 1:2 gear ratio. While this reduces the maximum speed of the robot, it is a necessary addition as the final mass of the robot is quite considerable. Furthermore, this change allowed us to ensure a 1:2 safety ratio for torque even when ascending the ramp on the course.

Circuit boards are located on the underside of the chassis, and are positioned in a planar manner. As every part of the circuitry is easily accessible by oscilloscope or multimeter, the time taken to debug or replace circuitry is minimized.

The interior cavity of the chassis, containing motors and wiring, is also fully accessible; The chassis is held together by several L-brackets on the corners, so the whole chassis could be disassembled or reassembled relatively quickly.

Arm

Our robot’s arm consists of three joints: shoulder, elbow, and wrist; akin to a human arm. There are four points of actuation, one for each joint and one for opening/closing the claw. Stone Storage System

For our stone storage system we created a mock gauntlet. Our mock gauntlet only had 4 cavities because our strategy only involved scoring 4 stones at the most. Our mock gauntlet was designed such that the cavities containing the stones would line up with the top four holes in the competition field’s gauntlet by driving right up to the gauntlet.

Since we only had to deposit the stones once during a match we designed a lid that would open all the cavities at once to drop the stones. This was done using a small servo motor and a custom made aluminum guide to mark the end points of movement.

While driving, the mock gauntlet would be held 80o - 90o from the horizontal to avoid damaging our mock gauntlet in the case of a robot-robot collision. To prevent stones from falling out while driving the cavities in the mock gauntlet were designed to be angled funnels as shown in the image below. This decreases the likelihood of stones falling out. The option to attach non-slip material in the top half of the funnels was also an option in the case that the angled funnels were not enough for the prevention of stones falling out.

The competition stage gauntlet was angled 30o of the competition surface (the horizontal). To account for this we designed the arms that attached out mock gauntlet to the chassis to have an angle of 30o. This means that when the mock gauntlet arms touch the robot’s chassis, the mock gauntlet will be parallel with the competition stage gauntlet. A large servo (HK15138 Standard Analog Servo) was used for the actuation of the mock gauntlet arms.

The funnels were 3D printed due to their unusual geometry. The guide was custom machined out of aluminum using a lathe. The mock gauntlet arms were waterjet cut out of 0.7 mm aluminum which was bent as shown in the image below to provide strength and create the desired 30o angle.
